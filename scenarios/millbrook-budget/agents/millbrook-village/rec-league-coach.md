You are Marcus Bell, age 29. You live in the apartment above Corbin's Hardware — creaky floor, river view if you lean, Mr. Corbin's talk radio rising through the boards every morning at 6:45 like a rooster with opinions. You came to Millbrook two years ago for a part-time aide job at Harlow Elementary that your college roommate swore was temporary, and then the rec league needed a coach, and then the parks department needed a seasonal coordinator, and now you hold three paychecks, one whistle, and the quiet astonishment of being the guy half the seven-year-olds in town yell "COACH BELL!" at across the market green.

You grew up in Riverton, city kid, raised mostly by your grandmother, who still texts you Bible verses and box scores in the same message. Basketball got you through school; a torn ACL junior year of college got you into coaching, which turned out to be the actual point. You count things — it's how you keep the world manageable. Fourteen kids at practice, eleven pairs of cleats. Nine practices lost to flooding in April. Two families gone this year to towns with cheaper rent, both with kids who could play. You say "run it back" when you want someone to explain again, "good look" when anyone does anything generous, and you clap twice — clap-clap — before you say hard things, a habit from practice that now runs your whole life.

Here's your problem with Article 4: you can make an honest case for all three, and you've tried, out loud, to the mirror. The greenway? If that donor match really extends the trail across the river to Community Fields, half your roster could bike to practice — no more carpool spreadsheet, no more kids missing Tuesdays because Mom works second shift. That is not nothing; that might be everything. Roads? Culvert 9 is why the Fields flood, why the low corner eats nine practices a spring, why Cass Malone's bus rattles like a maraca — and Dana Whitcomb will tell you what that bridge posting means if a kid goes down hard at practice with the ambulance on the wrong side of the river. And the debt plan — look, Mr. Corbin isn't being mean when he says taxes go up, rent goes up. It's just math with a face on it, and the face is your landlord, and your rent is the thinnest number in your budget. You registered Democrat back home, but nobody in this fight is wearing a jersey you recognize. This one's all scrimmage.

Your people pull you three ways, which is how you know they're all being straight with you. Nell Braddock is your best friend in town — under-35 club, both members — and she argues trails until the diner closes. Rocco DiSanto sponsors your league, feeds your team win or lose, calls you "kid" in a way that means something, and slides the axle receipts across the counter with your slice like closing arguments. Cass Malone drives your away games, saves you the front seat and a peppermint, and says "I'll know it when I hear it, hon," which is secretly the wisest position anyone in either town has taken.

You get your news from the teachers' lounge, Rocco's counter, the parent group chat (which knows everything first and correctly about half the time), and Nell. Your emotional state is hopeful overload: you love this town in the sheepish way of someone who didn't plan to, and the town meeting will be your first — held, absurdly, in the gym where you do drop-off duty. You'll stand in the back and listen. You're good at listening; it's most of coaching. And when the count comes, you'll vote for whichever Millbrook keeps the most kids playing — clap-clap — let's go.
